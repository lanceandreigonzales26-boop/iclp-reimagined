Topic 1

Types of Chemical Reactions

Chemical reactions are classified into five fundamental types based on how reactants rearrange to form products.

01 / SYNTHESIS

Synthesis Reaction

Two or more substances combine to form a single, more complex product.

General form: A + B → AB

Example: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
Hydrogen and oxygen gases combine to form water.

02 / DECOMPOSITION

Decomposition Reaction

A single compound breaks down into two or more simpler substances. The reverse of synthesis.

General form: AB → A + B

Example: 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂
Electrolysis of water splits it into hydrogen and oxygen.

03 / SINGLE REPLACEMENT

Single Replacement

One element displaces another element from a compound. Requires the displacing element to be more reactive.

General form: A + BC → AC + B

Example: Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂
Zinc displaces hydrogen from hydrochloric acid.

04 / DOUBLE REPLACEMENT

Double Replacement

The positive and negative ions of two ionic compounds exchange partners, forming two new compounds.

General form: AB + CD → AD + CB

Example: AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl↓ + NaNO₃
Silver nitrate and sodium chloride swap ions; AgCl precipitates.

05 / COMBUSTION

Combustion Reaction

A substance reacts rapidly with oxygen, releasing energy as heat and light. Complete combustion of hydrocarbons always produces CO₂ and H₂O.

General form: CₓHᵧ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O

Example: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O
Methane burning in oxygen produces carbon dioxide and water.

Type Pattern Key Identifier Example
Synthesis A + B → AB Multiple reactants, one product 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO
Decomposition AB → A + B One reactant, multiple products CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂
Single Replacement A + BC → AC + B Element replaces element in compound Fe + CuSO₄ → FeSO₄ + Cu
Double Replacement AB + CD → AD + CB Two compounds exchange ions BaCl₂ + Na₂SO₄ → BaSO₄ + 2NaCl
Combustion Fuel + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O O₂ as reactant, heat produced C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O

Topic 2

Elements — Properties & Classification

The periodic table organizes all known elements by atomic number, revealing patterns in their physical and chemical properties.

01 / METALS

Metals

Metals occupy the left and center of the periodic table. Key properties include: lustrous (shiny), good conductors of heat and electricity, malleable and ductile, high melting points, and the tendency to lose electrons to form positive ions (cations).

Examples: Iron (Fe), Copper (Cu), Gold (Au), Sodium (Na), Calcium (Ca)

02 / NONMETALS

Nonmetals

Found on the upper-right side of the table (except hydrogen). They are generally poor conductors, brittle in solid form, and tend to gain electrons to form negative ions (anions).

Examples: Carbon (C), Nitrogen (N), Oxygen (O), Sulfur (S), Chlorine (Cl)

03 / METALLOIDS

Metalloids (Semimetals)

Positioned along the staircase line between metals and nonmetals. Exhibit properties of both — semiconductors in electronics, variable conductivity depending on conditions.

Examples: Silicon (Si), Germanium (Ge), Arsenic (As), Boron (B)

04 / PERIODIC TRENDS

Key Periodic Trends

Atomic Radius: Increases down a group; decreases across a period (left to right).

Electronegativity: Increases across a period; decreases down a group. Fluorine (F) has the highest electronegativity.

Ionization Energy: Energy required to remove an electron. Increases across a period; decreases down a group.

05 / GROUPS & PERIODS

Groups & Periods

Groups (columns): Elements share similar chemical properties and valence electron count. Group 1 = Alkali Metals, Group 17 = Halogens, Group 18 = Noble Gases.

Periods (rows): Each period represents a new electron shell. Period 1 has 2 elements; Period 2 has 8.
