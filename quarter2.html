Topic 1

Law of Conservation of Mass

Matter cannot be created or destroyed in a chemical reaction — every atom on the left side of the arrow must appear on the right.

01 / FOUNDATION

What Conservation Means

In any chemical equation, the total number of atoms of each element must be equal on both sides of the reaction arrow. Chemical reactions rearrange atoms — they never create or destroy them.

An unbalanced equation is chemically incorrect; it implies atoms appear or vanish from nowhere.

02 / SUBSCRIPTS VS COEFFICIENTS

Subscripts & Coefficients

Subscripts (small numbers after an element symbol) indicate the number of atoms in a molecule — they are fixed and must never be changed to balance an equation.

Coefficients (large numbers in front of a formula) multiply the entire formula and are the only values you adjust when balancing.

Topic 2

Balancing Techniques

Two widely-used methods for balancing equations — choose whichever suits the complexity of the equation.

01 / TRIAL AND ERROR

Inspection Method

The most common approach. Follow these steps:

1. List atom counts on each side.
2. Balance metals first, then nonmetals, then hydrogen and oxygen last.
3. Adjust coefficients one compound at a time.
4. Verify by recounting all atoms.

Works well for straightforward equations; mastered through practice.

02 / STEP-BY-STEP EXAMPLE

Worked Example

Balance: Fe + O₂ → Fe₂O₃

Step 1 — Balance Fe: 2 atoms on right → put 4 in front of Fe.
4Fe + O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃

Step 2 — Balance O: 6 atoms on right → put 3 in front of O₂.
4Fe + 3O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃

Check: Fe: 4=4 ✓ | O: 6=6 ✓

03 / USEFUL TIPS

Balancing Tips

• If a polyatomic ion (like SO₄²⁻ or NO₃⁻) appears unchanged on both sides, treat it as a single unit.

• Balance oxygen and hydrogen last — they appear in many compounds and are easiest to fix at the end.

• If you get fractional coefficients, multiply the entire equation through by the denominator to clear fractions.

• Always reduce to the lowest whole-number ratio of coefficients.
